— II. The Model

How we cracked
the code.

The bank's proprietary system, treated as a black box, was opened with six per-segment, MAE-optimized gradient-boosted models trained on 231,276 historical calls — reproducing its outputs to within ~2.4% on average (graded 97.64%).

Active Loans — Ratio Targets

The breakthrough: predict VALUE ÷ a financial base, then multiply back — and train each model to minimize dollar error directly. On-time error fell from $327 to $84.

VALUE = model(features) × base

The model in one line: a separate specialist model for each of the six loan statuses, each a gradient-boosted tree ensemble tuned to minimise average dollar error — not statistical fit — and predicting the ratio of value to a financial base. We route each segment to its strongest model and average across random seeds for stability.

What "97.64% accuracy" means — precisely
The score is normalized mean absolute error: 1 − (MAE ÷ mean call value) = 1 − (≈$52.6 ÷ ≈$2,233) = 97.64% on the 25,698 valued calls. In plain terms: our prediction lands, on average, within ~2.4% of the bank's true value. It is not classification accuracy and not R². A key early lesson: optimizing for statistical fit (R²) actually scored worse (~90%) because it mispriced the extreme tails — so we rebuilt every model to minimize dollar error directly. Our cross-validation now tracks the live graded score to within ~0.02%.

— III. What It Values

What the algorithm
actually values.

By reverse-engineering 231,276 calls, we identified the true drivers of VALUE — and what they reveal about how the bank prices a loan.

Repayment History Dominates

The single most important signal is how much has already been repaid relative to the loan amount. Repayment ratio alone explains roughly 40% of VALUE variance in active loans — the bank is pricing expected future cash flows.

Outstanding Balance Sets the Floor

For on-time loans, VALUE is tightly bound to outstanding principal. The bank effectively prices the expected value of remaining repayments — discounted at an implicit rate we estimate at 14–16%.

Payment Status Is the Master Key

The most powerful single predictor is repayment status. It splits calls into populations whose VALUE ranges differ by orders of magnitude — from ~$55 for rejections to ~$68K for high-value performing loans.

What's Missing — External Data

The bank's model clearly draws on credit-bureau recovery rates and market-risk factors absent from the call ledger. This explains the remaining ~2.4% accuracy gap — and, plausibly, much of the demographic variation we observe.

The Customer Is Worth More Than the Loan

A performing loan is valued at roughly 1.35× its entire projected cash flow — about $4,190 of VALUE on ~$3,090 of expected payments. The bank is pricing the customer relationship (future borrowing and retention), not just the loan — so relationship depth, not loan size, is the clearest lever to raise value per future caller.

Top value drivers — relative feature importance

Importance is shown on a relative 0–100 scale (top driver = 100), derived from the active-loan models. It ranks drivers; it is not a percentage of VALUE.

— Practical Implication · Phase 3

Three levers to raise value per future caller

The drivers that explain VALUE also show where management can grow it — ranked by impact per call. Each assumes the remediated, fair model; none should be pursued through the protected-class patterns flagged in the audit.

01

Convert more applicants

A rejected call is worth ~$55; a performing loan ~$4,200 — an ~80× jump. Extending fair, risk-based approval to creditworthy applicants who are currently declined is the single biggest move on average value per caller.

02

Keep loans performing

Value swings by orders of magnitude with repayment status. Early-intervention collections and outreach that move borrowers from the delinquent band back to on-time protect the highest-value part of the book.

03

Deepen the relationship

A performing loan is valued at ~1.35× its own cash flow — about a third of its worth is the relationship. Cross-sell, retention and repeat lending grow that premium directly, lifting value per caller without a single larger loan.

— IV. Fairness Audit

The fairness
findings.

Why small differences still matter here

Under ECOA (15 U.S.C. § 1691), it is unlawful to let race, color, sex or national origin affect credit outcomes — and intent is not required. A neutral-looking model can still create unlawful disparate impact. That makes even small, systematic differences a compliance question, not just an economic one. The tests below are unadjusted — they do not yet control for credit risk — so they show where to look, not a final verdict.

Read significance and magnitude separately. With 200,000+ records, even microscopic differences register as "highly significant." Significance tells us a pattern is real, not noise; magnitude tells us how much it matters. Most gaps below are real but small — with one striking exception: the compulsory-insurance disparity, which is both highly significant and large.

★
The headline · tested from a team hypothesis

The disparity that matters: compulsory insurance

The demographic gap is not in the loan amount, and barely in the interest rate. It is in who gets compulsory insurance attached to their loan. Black-alone borrowers receive it 2.2× as often as White-alone borrowers — and the gap does not move when we compare people with identical credit scores.

z = 92 · p < 10⁻³⁰⁰

Loan amounts are essentially equal across races (a 2.6% spread). Insurance is not: White 22.0% · SOR 27.0% · Asian 26.3% · Black 48.9%. Mean credit scores are near-identical (696–699), and the ~27 pp Black–White gap persists in every credit-score quintile — so creditworthiness does not explain it.

Compulsory-insurance rate by race (%)
…and it holds at every credit-score level

If insurance were risk-based, these lines would slope with credit score. They are flat — the gap is by race, not risk.

Why this is the strongest finding — and what it means
Unlike the rate and VALUE gaps (real but tiny), this effect is large, adverse and robust. Compulsory insurance is an added cost and condition imposed on the borrower. Black-alone applicants carry it 2.2× as often (48.9% vs 22.0%) — a 27 pp gap that is stable across all five credit-score quintiles, and credit scores are near-identical across groups. Notably, for Black borrowers the rate rises from 37.2% at the initial offer to 48.9% in the final terms — insurance is added during finalisation — while it is unchanged for every other group. This is the clearest disparate-impact signal in the data and the one we would lead with. We have since traced the mechanism (next): it runs on national origin and, separately, race — surviving controls for credit score, nativity and geography. It remains observational, but the obvious legitimate explanations are already ruled out.
→
The mechanism · how insurance is assigned

Two protected dimensions, not one

We traced the rule. Compulsory insurance is assigned on two protected characteristics at once — national origin, and, separately, race.

Mechanism 1 — national origin

Every foreign-born borrower (100%) is placed on compulsory insurance, vs 16% of native-born. National origin is itself protected under ECOA.

Mechanism 2 — race, among native-born only

Holding nativity constant, only Black native-born borrowers are singled out — 4× every other native-born group.

Foreign-born share is near-identical for Black and White borrowers (13.5% vs 13.2%), so nativity explains essentially none of the race gap. The Black effect survives controls for both nativity and credit score — a direct race pattern, not a proxy. That leaves the board with two separate ECOA exposures: national origin and race.

01

Race tracks call VALUE

Average VALUE differs across racial groups by more than chance would produce.

F = 29.06 · p = 8.97×10⁻¹⁹

A difference this large would arise by chance less than once in a billion-billion samples — so the pattern is real. But VALUE is the bank's own valuation of the loan, not the borrower's cost, and it legitimately reflects repayment behaviour. Treat this as a flag for a controlled review, not as evidence of pricing bias.

Average VALUE by race ($)

Axis starts at $0 — the visible step is the true ~7.6% gap.

"White-alone calls carry the lowest average VALUE — an unmistakable statistical pattern, but one we cannot yet attribute to race."

The unadjusted gap between highest and lowest group is $171.88 per call. Median loan size is ~$1,900 across all groups, so loan size is not the driver — but credit risk, geography and sector remain uncontrolled.

Technical methodology
One-way ANOVA across four primary racial groups (n = 204,807 calls): F(3, 204803) = 29.06, p = 8.97×10⁻¹⁹. The gap is driven by lower average values for White-alone borrowers relative to Black, Asian, and Some-Other-Race groups. Loan-amount medians are ~$1,900 across all groups, ruling out loan size as a confound — but the test is univariate. Because VALUE is partly a function of realized repayment, the disparity may reflect differences in loan performance and could also encode information through proxies (geography, employment sector, credit score). A multivariate model controlling for credit risk is required to separate these explanations.

How a model with no "race" or "gender" field can still produce these gaps
None of these protected attributes appear in Siren Capital's disclosed model features. Differences can still arise because seemingly neutral variables act as proxies: zip code correlates with race; some credit-history variables can carry the legacy of historical redlining; employment sector correlates with gender. A model that never "sees" race can therefore still reproduce racial patterns through correlated inputs (the omitted-variable / proxy problem). This is exactly why disparate-impact law does not require intent — and why the fix is a controlled audit of which inputs carry protected information, not an assumption of bad faith.
What we would check next — to confirm or clear each flag
1 — A controlled, multivariate analysis that holds credit risk (credit score, debt-to-income, geography) constant. This is the single most important next step before any conclusion. We also screened two further ECOA-protected classes — marital status and age: marital status shows a modest, likely age-driven rate spread (never-married ~14.7% vs widowed ~15.6%, ~0.9 pp), folded into the same controlled review.

2 — TESTED ✓ Confirmed and traced. The disparity is in compulsory insurance, not the loan amount, and runs on two protected dimensions — national origin (100% of foreign-born) and race (native-born Black 4×). It survives credit-score, nativity and geography controls (see the headline finding). Remaining: loan-purpose and property-value checks would only further isolate it.

3 — Robustness to multiple-comparison corrections and to alternative model specifications.

4 — Whether the race–VALUE gap survives once realized repayment behaviour is controlled for, given that VALUE is partly a function of repayment.

— VIII. Ethical Framework

Our commitment
to responsible analysis.

How we conducted this work, who is affected by what we found — and by what we recommend — and what obligations flow from it, for us and for the board.

I — Methodology

Methodological Integrity

Established tests throughout: ANOVA for group VALUE differences, Chi-square for categorical associations, two-sample t-tests for continuous outcomes, with full statistics reported. Given the large sample, we report uncorrected p-values but emphasise effect sizes — and note that even under conservative multiple-comparison corrections, the race and acceptance findings remain significant.

II — Affected Parties

Who Is Affected — by the findings

Most directly affected: foreign-born borrowers — all 33,228 are forced onto compulsory insurance (100%) — and native-born Black borrowers, charged it 4× as often as other native-born groups; neither is explained by credit score. Both are ECOA-protected (national origin and race). More modestly, 65,922 accepted women paid ~$1.60/year more on average, and acceptance varied 3.4 pp by race. The acquirer inherits the legal, financial and reputational exposure.

III — Affected Parties

Who Is Affected — if you act

Acting on our recommendations helps borrowers (remediation, fairer pricing) — but has tensions to manage. Remediation and monitoring require processing protected-class data, which must be tightly governed. And a stricter acceptance bar applied bluntly could reduce access. Fixes must be designed, not just switched on.

IV — Data Privacy

Privacy Protection

All individual-level work was on aggregated outputs. No PII — names, SSNs, card numbers, IP addresses, passport numbers — was accessed or retained; PII columns were excluded at preprocessing, before any modeling. Demographic fields were used only for the fairness audit, not for prediction.

V — Dual Use & Safeguards

Safeguards on Our Model

The reverse-engineered model is itself a powerful artifact. We recommend it be access-controlled, not deployed for production pricing until the fairness audit clears it, kept under human oversight, and shipped with documented limitations. We disclose its accuracy and its weaknesses (e.g. delinquent loans) in full.

VI — What Fair Means

There Is No Single "Fair"

Fairness has competing, mathematically incompatible definitions — demographic parity, equal opportunity, and calibration cannot all hold at once when base rates differ. The board must choose a standard. We recommend: equal treatment conditional on legitimate risk factors, backed by ongoing disparate-impact monitoring — and we name the trade-off rather than hide it.

A note on the limits of this analysis — Our findings come from observational data and univariate tests. We cannot rule out that the disparities are explained by legitimate credit-risk factors absent from the call ledger (credit scores, debt-to-income, etc.), and we do not claim to have proven discrimination. What we can say is that the patterns are statistically robust and systematic along protected lines. Given what is at stake in this transaction, the prudent commercial posture is to require Siren Capital to evidence a risk-based justification as a condition of closing — rather than for the board to assume innocence — and to confirm or clear each flag with a controlled audit on complete credit data.
